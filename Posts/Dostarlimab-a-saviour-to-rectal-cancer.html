Dostarlimab; a saviour to rectal cancer

Introduction

Dostarlimab is an experimental drug sold under the brand name Jemperli. It contains laboratory-produced molecules and acts as substitute antibodies. It is also used to treat certain types of solid tumors in the rectum that have spread to other parts of the body in adults who failed to respond to a previous chemotherapy medication and do not have any other effective treatment options.

Symptoms

Dostarlimab-gxly may cause side effects similar to the following:

  • cough, chest pain, or shortness of breath
  • diarrhea; increase in number of bowel movements; black, tarry, sticky stools, or stools that have blood or mucus in them; or stomach-area pain or tenderness
  • yellowing of skin or eyes, dark-colored urine, bleeding or bruising more easily than normal, loss of appetite, severe nausea or vomiting, decreased energy, or pain on right side of stomach area
  • headaches, including those that are unusual or will not go away.

How did dostarlimab cure rectal cancer?

A clinical trial took place wherein, patients of rectum cancer, took Dostarlimab every three weeks for six months. "It is noteworthy that they were all in similar stages of their cancer. The cancer was locally advanced in the rectum but had not spread to other organs," added doctors, who had taken part in reporting the trial.

Patients involved in this trial had received chemoradiotherapy or undergone surgery, that could result in complications such as bowel, urinary, and even sexual dysfunction. The 18 patients expected to have to go through these surgeries as the next step in the research. However, they were surprised to learn that no more therapy was required. Rectum cancer is undetectable by physical exam; endoscopy; PET scans or MRI scans. This proves that Dostarlimab can be a 'potential' cure for one of the most deadly common cancers.

Conclusion

Experts praised the study because not all of the participants experienced serious side effects from the medication trial.

Separately, Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center and a co-author of the paper, oncologist Dr. Andrea Cercek, described the moment patients found out they were cancer-free. “There were a lot of happy tears,” she exclaimed. According to cancer researchers who studied the medicine, it appears to be promising, but a larger-scale trial is required.
