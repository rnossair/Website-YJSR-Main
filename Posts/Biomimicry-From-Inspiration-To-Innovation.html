Biomimicry

From Inspiration To Innovation

Posted by Swarna Sre on August 15, 2023 Edited by Zhuri Burchenson

Abstract

It is evident the kind of intricate systems nature encompasses. From the seamless efficiency of a spider’s web to the complex patterns on the butterfly's wings, nature has always been a prodigious designer, engineer, and architect, refining its skills over billions of years to find the best ways for its own survival, as well as the flourishing of the ecosystem as a whole.

Biomimicry is the concept of drawing inspiration from time-tested skills, designs and strategies , implemented by nature for years and using it to create innovations, applying such to human ideas and requirements.

The wonders of nature have captivated the human imagination since time immemorial. The Wright brothers found inspiration and key to their soaring dreams (quite literally) in the hands of nature - birds and aerial creatures. Another famous story of innovation through biomimicry - Velcro from Burdock Seeds. Swiss engineer George de Mestral, inspired by burdock seeds sticking to his clothes and his dog's fur, invented Velcro, the hook-and-loop fastening system used in various applications, including clothing, shoes, and medical devices. This is just the beginning of what Biomimicry could mean. Although the concept is being widely explored and gaining popularity, the potential it holds in a sustainable future is still majorly contained.

The word ‘Biomimicry’ is primarily derived from two Greek words, “bios” meaning life, and “mimesis” meaning ‘to imitate’. This multidisciplinary approach, drawing inspiration from biological systems and processes, is aimed to design and engineer ingenious solutions to improve our daily-lives, answer pressing challenges of today, and provide alternatives. By studying the principles behind nature's designs, scientists gain insights into the structures and processes that have withstood time and evolution.

Biomimicry and Sustainability

Nature has used the trial and error method for billions of years, continuously producing a better version of itself, efficiently, and most definitely, sustainably. Using methods approved by nature backs up the idea behind it for the same reasons.

A good example is the beehive, where bees have crafted a remarkable system that optimises energy efficiency, material usage, and waste reduction, providing valuable lessons for sustainable human practices. Honeybees construct intricate hexagonal honeycomb structures, maximising structural stability, effective space and resource utilisation. Drawing inspiration from these beehive designs, architects and engineers can develop sustainable building designs and cellular structures in material science.

Biomimicry can also contribute to sustainability by reducing damage to the environment, improving resource efficiency, and promoting a more harmonious relationship between human activities and nature. Using methods that have proven to be the best way to keep both work and sustainability in mind, would help in meeting the ever-increasing demand for innovations as the years pass, while also addressing the need to keep them eco-friendly.

Biomimicry in Application

Numerous applications of biomimicry are currently in practice. From modern turbine blades (mirroring energy consumptions of humpback whales) to bullet trains (by mimicing kingfishers) and from needles (emulating mosquitoes) to swim-suits (mirroring the skins of sharks), the list of inventive manifestations of nature is boundless. Some of them are explained in detail below:

  • Sonar:
    • Drawing inspiration from the natural sonar - short for ‘SOund NAvigation and Ranging’ - and echolocation systems including signal processing and sensing adaptability, researchers developed innovative technologies and applications in various fields.
  • Water-Repellent Effect:
    • The lotus leaf possesses a unique nano and micro-scale hierarchical structure on its surface, such that when water comes in contact with the waxed-like hydrophobic surface, it effortlessly rolls off. The lotus leaf's ability to repel water and self-clean through its surface, known as the lotus effect, has inspired biomimicry in material science and surface engineering, leading to the development of superhydrophobic and self-cleaning materials used in paints, coatings, and textiles.
  • Advances in Optics:
    • The photonic crystals found in certain butterfly wings have led to advancements in the fields of optics and material science. These photonic crystals are microscopic structures that diffract light, creating iridescent colours on the wings of butterflies, and producing stunning displays. Understanding these natural photonic crystals has guided researchers in developing innovative optical devices and advanced materials.
  • Light Without Heat:
    • The phenomenon of bioluminescence, where light is emitted through biochemical reactions, is observed in nature through some fungi, deep-sea creatures and fireflies. The light produced is typically cold or non-thermal, meaning the biochemical process does not produce heat, making it an incredibly energy efficient way to create light. This phenomenon inspired scientists to explore methods to mimic such solutions.
  • Ventilation Systems:
    • The elaborate structures found in termite mounds are highly impressive in that they maintain a stable internal temperature. Even in a hot and arid environment, which is crucial for the survival of the termite colony. Architects and engineers have sought to emulate this marvel to create more sustainable building designs, saving on artificial resources and using natural ones instead.

Summary

At the heart of Biomimicry lies a profound understanding of how living organisms in diverse ecosystems have evolved to optimise their survival in this interconnected world. By uncovering the workings behind the genius of nature’s inventions, scientists are uncovering a treasure trove of resilient, sustainable ideas that are sure to revolutionise our modern world. The aforementioned innovations and ideas drawn from the habits of nature for improvements in man-made applications are just a few of the examples of biomimicry that are being researched to occupy a more active role of usage in our daily lives. As the world grapples with ecological challenges, biomimicry persists as a beam of hope on the grounds of technologies, providing a steady and tested pathway right from inspiration to innovation!
